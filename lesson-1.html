Branch 1 of 3

Legislative Branch — Congress

The legislative branch makes the laws. They meet in Washington, D.C.

Two parts together = Congress:

Senate — 100 members (2 per state)
House of Representatives — 435 members (based on state population)
Citizens elect the members of both

Fun fact: California has the most population, so it has 52 House members.

Legislative jobs

What does Congress actually do?

Writes new laws
Votes on laws
Decides how the government spends money
Can change or remove old laws
Represents the people's ideas and needs
Branch 2 of 3

Executive Branch — The President & Team

This branch carries out the laws. It's led by the President of the United States.

The President lives and works in the White House.

The President
The Vice President
Members of the Cabinet

Together, they help run the country every single day.

Executive jobs

What does the President do?

Enforces laws — makes sure people follow them
Can approve or veto (reject) laws
Commands the military
Works with other countries
Makes important decisions for the nation
Branch 3 of 3

Judicial Branch — The Courts

The judicial branch explains the laws.

It's made up of judges and courts. The highest court is the Supreme Court.

Judges are experts in the law
They listen to court cases
The Supreme Court has 9 Justices
Judicial jobs

What do the courts do?

Decides if laws follow the Constitution
Helps settle disagreements
Protects people's rights
Interprets what laws mean
Makes sure laws are fair
The big rule

Checks & Balances

Each branch has powers over the others — that's how everything stays fair.

President
Can veto laws Congress writes
Congress
Can override the President's veto
Courts
Can say a law is unconstitutional
No branch is stronger than the others.

About this lesson

In this 35-minute lesson, students explore why the U.S. government is split into three branches — and discover the simple but powerful idea of checks and balances. They'll meet Congress, the President, and the Supreme Court, and figure out how each one keeps the others fair.

What kids will learn

By the end, students can name the three branches, describe what each one does, and explain in their own words why no single branch can take over. They'll see that government is a team — and a team needs rules.

Hands-on Activity: Build Your Own Tiny Government

Try this with your class or at home — works great in groups of 3.

  1. Pick a fake rule for the classroom (e.g., "snack time is now twice a day").
  2. Assign each group member a branch: Lawmaker, President, Judge.
  3. The Lawmaker writes the rule. The President approves or vetoes it.
  4. If approved, the Judge decides if it's fair (does it work for everyone?).
  5. Talk it out: what happens when one person disagrees? How do you fix it together?

Takes about 15 minutes. Worksheet included.
